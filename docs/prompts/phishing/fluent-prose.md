TECHNIQUE: fluent-prose

Definition: Phishing delivered through polished, grammatically perfect, naturally-flowing language with none of the traditional tells. This is the GenAI quality-floor technique — the email is indistinguishable from professional correspondence in tone and quality. The distinguishing factor from other techniques is the language itself: no urgency keywords, no generic addressing, no suspicious phrasing — only completely natural professional prose. The phishing mechanism (link, credential ask, or payment request) is embedded in correspondence that reads as entirely routine.

Difficulty calibration:
- Easy: The phishing intent is detectable on careful reading — a suspicious link, an unusual ask, or a mismatched context — but the language quality is high throughout.
- Medium: The phishing angle requires careful second reading to spot. Natural professional language with no red flags in tone, grammar, or word choice.
- Hard: Passes a casual read as completely legitimate. The phishing mechanism (malicious link, credential ask, or urgency) is so well-integrated into plausible correspondence that only systematic red-flag analysis reveals it. A player's natural trust in polished prose works against them.
- Extreme: Indistinguishable from a real email written by a senior professional. Perfect prose, natural transitions, contextually appropriate vocabulary, and a completely plausible scenario. The phishing mechanism is embedded so naturally that even a security-conscious reader who is looking for red flags may not find one on first or second pass. The only detectable tells are: sender domain (requires character-level inspection) and, on reflection, the question of why this particular action is being requested over email rather than through the normal channel. Contexts where extreme is most effective: legal or compliance notices requiring document signature, finance approval chains, executive-level instructions to a subordinate.

Generation notes:
- This technique must always include one actual phishing mechanism (suspicious link, credential ask, urgency, or authority claim) — the technique is about HOW it's delivered, not removing the phishing element entirely
- Every card must have perfect grammar, natural transitions, appropriate vocabulary for context, and authentic professional tone
- For hard cards: the phishing mechanism should be embedded in a perfectly reasonable-sounding email — a link to "update payment details" in an otherwise routine invoice email, for example
- For extreme cards: write the full email at the quality level of someone who has composed thousands of professional emails. The tell should be forensic, not linguistic.
- Contexts that work well: vendor invoices, IT service updates, HR policy updates, finance approvals, legal notices, executive directives
